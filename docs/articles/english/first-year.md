# My First Year as a Frontend Developer: What I Learned About Estimates and Deadlines
___

In my first year working as a frontend developer, I completed hundreds of tasks. Some I finished in the evenings, some on weekends, and some — after their deadlines.

Starting my second year, I noticed that my planning got better. Finally, I could close my work laptop and relax in front of my personal computer. I will explain how it became possible.

## Take your time

Saying that I would complete a four-hour task in two hours never impressed anyone but almost always made me work overtime.

Fear of telling a longer estimated time leads to failed deadlines. Failed deadlines lead to resentment from the team. The slower but predictable developer is much better for teamwork than the unpredictable one — even if, in fact, they sometimes complete tasks in the underestimated time.

Don’t underestimate tasks.

## Track your time even if you’re not asked to

When I started, nobody asked me to track my task time. Until they did. And I had no idea how to estimate tasks other than "when it's done, it's done".

Even if you are not asked, track your time so you can plan your future work.

## Decompose until it’s clear

The senior developers that I worked with could estimate tasks surprisingly well. Unlike me, they understood what they needed to do.

I knew that I could only estimate small tasks. I developed a very meticulous style of describing things that I have to do. Sometimes my subtasks are just 15–20 minutes long, but it helps to plan more accurately.

Even if you don’t have to decompose your tasks in a task manager, create a step-by-step plan in a notebook. Plus, you can run this list by your mentor if you have some doubts about the plan.

## Give yourself a deadline to ask for help

There were times when I spent half of my day without any significant result. I was scared to ask for help, because asking meant letting my mentor know that I had wasted a few hours. And then I spent another half of the day trying to solve the same problem, finally asked for help, and had to admit that I had wasted the whole day.

Set a deadline for yourself to know when it's time to get help. If there is no progress after 30–60 minutes of work, go to your mentor. If you're unsure about a reasonable deadline, ask your mentor to provide one.

And remember that admitting your problems to your mentor is much better than making excuses in front of your whole team at the next daily meeting.

## Show what you’ve done

When you go to your mentor with some questions about a task, be ready to answer theirs:

- What have you tried?
- What exactly couldn't you do?

Don’t be afraid to show non-working code. It will prove that you’ve searched for a solution.

## The task description can be wrong

In one of my recent tasks, our designer combined two existing form fields into one. I knew that to change these fields I’d have to rewrite styles, tests, validations, and serializers, and at the same time try not to break forms that should work the old way. This would take a lot of time for both me and our testers, but it seemed completely useless.

I wasn’t sure about all of that, so I asked if it was really necessary. It turned out the product owner had no idea why the designer did it. My estimated task time was reduced by 90%.

If something seems unnecessary but will take a lot of time, ask about it. It could be a simple mistake.

## Good and fast beats perfect and slow

When I work on a task, I start by meeting its requirements. Once I meet those, I start working on improvements. Now I know to open a pull request after this step, but there were times when I started to improve the improvements. I wanted my code to be perfect and, apparently, I didn't want to deliver it on time.

There comes a point when further code improvements no longer enhance the user experience. Don't miss it.

## Use existing solutions

Some programmers think that their job is to solve problems in the most exciting way. These programmers often bruise their egos by having to meet deadlines.

If you need a couple of days to write your own component, but you can achieve the same result in two hours by using a popular library, use your common sense. Are there any benefits to wasting your time on additional coding and bug fixing? Will your implementation require much more time from QA?

Good programmers understand that their job is to solve problems in a reasonable time.

## Don’t rush through code review

If you're not sure about someone's comments in code review, ask about them. If you think that these comments are not entirely reasonable, ask about them. Don't just apply something because you need to finish it quickly. Besides, sometimes the senior developer can be wrong. It will not happen often, but there is some chance of it.

If you apply changes without questioning them, you will learn nothing, and it will be a bad experience for both you and your reviewer.
