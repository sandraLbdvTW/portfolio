# My First Year as a Frontend Developer: What I Learned About Estimates and Deadlines
___

:::note
I wrote the original version of this article many years ago, after my first year as a frontend developer.
Since then, I've changed careers and no longer work as a programmer, but the principles it describes—realistic estimates, early questions, and predictable delivery—still guide how I work today.
:::

In my first year as a frontend developer, I completed hundreds of tasks. I finished some in the evenings, some on weekends, and some long after their deadlines.

At the start of my second year, I noticed that my planning had improved. Finally, I could close my work laptop and relax in front of my personal computer. Here is how that became possible.

## Take your time

Promising to complete a four-hour task in two hours never impressed anyone, but it almost always made me work overtime.

Fear of giving a higher estimate leads to missed deadlines. Missed deadlines lead to resentment within the team. A slower but predictable developer is much better for teamwork than an unpredictable one—even if the latter manages to meet an overly optimistic estimate from time to time.

Don’t underestimate tasks.

## Track your time even if you’re not asked to

When I started, nobody asked me to track the time I spent on tasks—until they did. I had no idea how to estimate them other than "when it's done, it's done."

Even if you aren’t asked to, track your time so you can plan future work.

## Decompose until it’s clear

The senior developers I worked with could estimate tasks surprisingly well. Unlike me, they understood what they needed to do.

I knew that I could estimate only small tasks. I developed a very meticulous way of describing what I had to do. Sometimes my subtasks are only 15–20 minutes long, but this helps me plan more accurately.

Even if you don’t have to decompose your tasks in a task manager, create a step-by-step plan in a notebook. You can also run this list by your mentor if you have any doubts about the plan.

## Give yourself a deadline to ask for help

There were times when I spent half the day without making any significant progress. I was afraid to ask for help because asking meant letting my mentor know that I had wasted a few hours. Then I spent the other half of the day trying to solve the same problem, finally asked for help, and had to admit that I had wasted the whole day.

Set yourself a deadline for asking for help. If you make no progress after 30–60 minutes of work, go to your mentor. If you're unsure what a reasonable deadline would be, ask your mentor to suggest one.

And remember that admitting your problems to your mentor is much better than making excuses in front of your whole team at the next daily meeting.

## Show what you’ve done

When you go to your mentor with questions about a task, be ready to answer theirs:

- What have you tried?
- Where exactly did you get stuck?

Don’t be afraid to show code that doesn’t work. It will prove that you’ve looked for a solution.

## The task description can be wrong

In one of my recent tasks, our designer combined two existing form fields into one. I knew that changing these fields would require me to rewrite styles, tests, validations, and serializers while trying not to break forms that still had to work the old way. This would take a lot of time for both me and our testers, but the change seemed completely useless.

I wasn’t sure about any of this, so I asked whether it was really necessary. It turned out that the product owner had no idea why the designer had done it, so the change was dropped — and my estimate for the task fell by 90%.

If something seems unnecessary but would take a lot of time, ask about it. It might be a simple mistake.

## Good and fast beats perfect and slow

When I work on a task, I start by meeting its requirements. Once I have met them, I start working on improvements. Now I know to open a pull request at this point, but there were times when I started improving the improvements. I wanted my code to be perfect and apparently didn’t want to deliver it on time.

There comes a point when further code improvements no longer enhance the user experience. Don't miss it.

## Use existing solutions

Some programmers think that their job is to solve problems in the most exciting way. Writing a component from scratch is often more fun than installing one. Deadlines, regrettably, are indifferent to how much fun I’m having.

If writing your own component would take a couple of days, but using a popular library would achieve the same result in two hours, use common sense. Is the extra coding and bug fixing worth your time? Would your implementation require much more time from QA?

Good programmers understand that their job is to solve problems in a reasonable time.

## Don’t rush through code review

If you're not sure about someone's code review comments, ask about them. If you think the comments aren't entirely reasonable, say so. Don't apply a change just because you need to finish quickly. After all, even a senior developer can be wrong. It will not happen often, but the possibility exists.

If you apply changes without questioning them, you will learn nothing, and it will be a bad experience for both you and your reviewer.

## Predictability beats heroics

I didn't get better at meeting deadlines by learning to code twice as fast. I got better at seeing the work more clearly, asking questions earlier, and stopping before useful improvements turned into endless polishing.

I still underestimate tasks and get stuck sometimes. The difference is that I notice sooner—and most days, that's enough for the evening to stay mine.
